01 Project Proposal and User Research 20% ▼
1a. Innovation and Concept Sub-weight: 7 / 20 pts
Excellent
90–100%
Proposes a genuinely novel interaction technique or interface with a clearly articulated gap in existing solutions. The real-world problem is specific, scoped, and validated with preliminary evidence (literature, observed behavior). Innovation is distinct from superficial feature additions.
Good
75–89%
Concept addresses a real problem with a meaningful interaction novelty. The gap in existing solutions is identified but may rely partly on assertion rather than evidence. Innovation direction is clear and plausible, even if not fully differentiated.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Concept is functional and well-intentioned but represents an incremental or derivative approach. Novelty claims are not strongly justified. Problem may be too broad or too vague to guide research meaningfully.
Needs Improvement
<60%
Concept replicates an existing product without meaningful differentiation. No novel interaction technique is proposed. Problem is abstract, assumed, or misaligned with HCI scope (e.g., purely a backend engineering problem).

1b. User Empathy and Understanding Sub-weight: 7 / 20 pts
Excellent
90–100%
Conducts 4+ interviews with representative users; transcripts or detailed notes provided. Empathy maps synthesize emotional, behavioral, and attitudinal dimensions. Personas derived from grouped, cross-participant patterns — not individual profiles or invented archetypes. Direct evidence traces back to persona attributes.
Good
75–89%
Conducts 3+ interviews with appropriate participants. Empathy maps are present and mostly complete but may conflate assumptions with observed data. Personas are plausible and pattern-based, though some attributes lack explicit research grounding.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Fewer than 3 interviews, or participants are not representative (e.g., convenience sample of classmates for a specialized domain). Empathy maps present but shallow. Personas feel prescriptive or assumption-driven rather than evidence-derived.
Needs Improvement
<60%
No user interviews conducted. Personas are entirely fictional or drawn from personal experience alone. No empathy maps, or maps are populated with unsupported generalizations. Research artifacts appear fabricated or retroactively constructed.

Grader notes — 1b
1c. Problem Definition Sub-weight: 6 / 20 pts
Excellent
90–100%
Journey maps trace a real user's experience end-to-end with explicit emotional arcs; pain points identified at specific touchpoints. Problem statement follows a testable format ("[User] needs [goal] because [insight]"). Hypothesis and goal statements are measurable and research-grounded. Zero unattributed assumptions.
Good
75–89%
Journey maps are present and mostly accurate. Problem statement is clear and user-centered but may contain one or two implicit assumptions. Goal and hypothesis statements are present and largely measurable, with minor gaps in research traceability.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Journey maps exist but may be incomplete or generic. Problem statement is vague or solution-framing ("users need our app"). Hypothesis or goal statements are missing or untestable. Some pain points appear assumed rather than observed.
Needs Improvement
<60%
No journey maps, or maps describe an idealized rather than real user experience. Problem statement is absent, circular, or solution-first. Hypothesis is missing. Definitions rest entirely on designer assumptions with no user evidence cited.
1c — Assign performance level:
or enter score:
Grader notes — 1c
Common Pitfalls — Section 1
Weak research: fewer than 3 users, friends/family for a specialized population, or surveys substituted for contextual interviews. Performative empathy maps that list demographics rather than attitudes and behaviors. Personas labeled "tech-savvy millennial" with no research basis are penalized as assumptions. Problem statements that describe a solution ("we will build an app that…") rather than a user need are considered misdefined and should be flagged explicitly.
02 Ideation and Low-Fidelity Prototyping 20% ▼
2a. Ideation Process Sub-weight: 7 / 20 pts
Excellent
90–100%
Competitive audit covers 4+ direct and indirect competitors with structured evaluation across consistent dimensions. Gaps are explicitly identified. HMW questions are reframed from specific user research pain points — not generic brainstorming prompts — and clearly link to subsequent design decisions.
Good
75–89%
Competitive audit covers 3+ competitors with reasonable consistency. HMW questions are present and mostly derived from research, though some may be broad or weakly connected to identified pain points. Design direction is traceable to ideation output.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Competitive audit exists but is superficial (screenshots only, no analytical framework). HMW questions are present but feel generic or unconnected to actual research findings. Link between ideation and design choices is unclear.
Needs Improvement
<60%
No competitive audit, or audit covers only 1–2 products with no evaluative framework. HMW questions are absent or simply restate the problem. Ideation lacks any structured methodology; designs appear conceived before research was complete.

Grader notes — 2a
2b. Information Architecture Sub-weight: 6 / 20 pts
Excellent
90–100%
User flows cover primary and edge-case paths with clear decision nodes. Storyboards depict realistic context of use and emotional states, not just screen transitions. Sitemap is hierarchically accurate, consistent with the prototype, and reflects the mental model derived from research.
Good
75–89%
User flows cover primary paths; edge cases may be missing. Storyboards are present and contextually grounded. Sitemap is consistent with wireframes but may have minor structural gaps or labeling inconsistencies.
Satisfactory
60–74%
User flows cover only the "happy path" with no branching. Storyboards are linear screen-dumps without context or emotion. Sitemap exists but does not match the prototype structure, suggesting it was produced in isolation.
Needs Improvement
<60%
One or more of user flows, storyboards, or sitemap is missing. What is present is incomplete, inaccurate, or copied from an unrelated template. IA artifacts do not connect to the proposed design.

Grader notes — 2b
2c. Wireframing Sub-weight: 7 / 20 pts
Excellent
90–100%
Paper sketches show genuine exploratory iteration (multiple divergent concepts visible). Digital wireframes use standard lo-fi symbols consistently across all screens. Interactive navigation between at least 5 key screens is functional. No high-fidelity styling in lo-fi phase.
Good
75–89%
Paper sketches show 2+ variations per key screen. Digital wireframes follow lo-fi conventions with minor deviations. Navigation between major screens works. Some non-standard symbols appear but do not impede comprehension.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Paper sketches exist but show a single concept per screen. Digital wireframes include prematurely applied styling (fonts, colors) that conflates lo-fi and hi-fi phases. Navigation is minimal or non-functional.
Needs Improvement
<60%
Paper sketches are absent or are post-hoc drawings of a digital prototype already built. Digital wireframes are missing or are screenshots of competitor products. No interactive navigation present.
2c — Assign performance level:
or enter score:
Grader notes — 2c
Common Pitfalls — Section 2
Fabricated HMW questions not derived from documented pain points. Wireframes that are screenshots of existing apps relabeled. Lo-fi prototypes with full color palettes and real typography are not lo-fi — flag as non-compliant. Sitemaps submitted after hi-fi prototypes that reflect the final design rather than the planned architecture are treated as reverse-engineered artifacts.
03 Usability Testing and Iteration 20% ▼
3a. Testing ExecutionSub-weight: 7 / 20 pts
Excellent
90–100%
Recruits 4–5 users matching the target persona; recruitment rationale documented. Test protocol includes task scenarios, think-aloud prompts, and observation notes. Both usability issues and innovation-specific feedback are captured. Raw observation notes or recordings are archived.
Good
75–89%
Tests with 3–4 appropriate users. Protocol is structured but may lack think-aloud prompting. Qualitative data is collected; both usability and some innovation-relevant feedback are documented. Minor gaps in participant representativeness are acknowledged.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Tests with 3 users, at least some of whom do not match the target persona. Protocol is informal. Feedback collected is mostly positive and lacks critical usability insight. Testing appears confirmatory rather than exploratory.
Needs Improvement
<60%
Fewer than 3 users tested, or testing is undocumented. Participants are clearly non-representative. No structured protocol evident. Feedback is anecdotal ("they liked it") with no specific behavioral observations recorded.

Grader notes — 3a
3b. Synthesizing InsightsSub-weight: 6 / 20 pts
Excellent
90–100%
Affinity diagram constructed from individual raw observations; clusters labeled with insight statements, not topic nouns. P0/P1/P2 uses an explicit rationale (frequency × severity). All P0 and P1 issues have direct traceability to observed test moments.
Good
75–89%
Affinity diagram present with reasonable clustering. Insight labels are mostly pattern-based but some clusters use generic topic labels. P0/P1/P2 is applied with a stated rationale, though prioritization logic may be partially implicit.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Affinity diagram uses broad category labels (e.g., "navigation," "visuals") without actionable insight statements. P0/P1/P2 assigned but without visible rationale — prioritization appears arbitrary.
Needs Improvement
<60%
No affinity diagram, or a list formatted in columns rather than a diagram. P0/P1/P2 framework absent or misapplied (e.g., all issues labeled P0). Insights not traceable to specific test observations.

Grader notes — 3b
3c. Design IterationSub-weight: 7 / 20 pts
Excellent
90–100%
All P0 and P1 issues addressed with documented before/after comparisons. Each change annotated with rationale linking to a specific insight. P2 changes implemented where feasible and acknowledged where deferred with justification. Design evolution clearly visible.
Good
75–89%
All P0 issues and most P1 issues addressed. Before/after documentation exists for major changes. Rationale provided for most changes but may be missing for 1–2 minor ones. P2 items are acknowledged.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Some P0 issues remain unaddressed without justification. Before/after documentation incomplete or shows cosmetic changes only. Design evolution is difficult to distinguish from the lo-fi version.
Needs Improvement
<60%
No documented iteration. Hi-fi prototype does not reflect usability testing findings. Before/after documentation absent or shows no meaningful changes. Team appears to have proceeded from lo-fi to hi-fi without an iteration cycle.
3c — Assign performance level:
or enter score:
Grader notes — 3c
Common Pitfalls — Section 3
Confirmatory testing: designing tests that only verify what works. A usability test producing zero P0 issues for a first-draft prototype is almost always a sign of leading questions or non-representative participants. Affinity diagrams that are lists in columns are not diagrams. "Before vs. after" showing only a color change with no structural improvement will be scored as non-iterative.
04 High-Fidelity Prototyping and Accessibility 25% ▼
4a. Visual DesignSub-weight: 9 / 25 pts
Excellent
90–100%
Typography uses a deliberate scale (≥3 levels) with consistent hierarchy. Color system includes primary, secondary, neutral, and semantic palette defined in Figma styles/variables. Gestalt principles are demonstrably applied — not simply named. A design system (components, auto-layout, shared styles) is evident.
Good
75–89%
Visual hierarchy is generally clear. Color palette is intentional and mostly consistent. Some Gestalt principles applied but not all demonstrable across screens. Minor inconsistencies in component sizing, alignment, or spacing exist but do not disrupt comprehension.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Prototype is visually functional but lacks a coherent design system. Typography is inconsistent across screens. Color choices appear arbitrary. Gestalt principles invoked in the report but not visibly applied. Spacing and alignment are inconsistent.
Needs Improvement
<60%
Prototype resembles a wireframe with colors applied rather than a hi-fi design. No typographic scale. Color usage inconsistent or inaccessible. Screens appear individually designed with no unifying visual language.

Grader notes — 4a
4b. Novel Interaction DemonstrationSub-weight: 9 / 25 pts
Excellent
90–100%
Innovative interaction communicated unambiguously through animations, annotated interaction flow, or narrated demo video. An observer unfamiliar with the project can understand what is novel, how it works, and why it improves on conventional alternatives. Interaction feedback states (active, error, success) are prototyped.
Good
75–89%
Novel interaction is present and visible in the prototype or video. The mechanism is mostly clear to an observer, though some steps may require verbal explanation. Key states are prototyped; edge states (error, empty) may be missing.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Novel interaction is claimed but only partially prototyped. A static screen is presented where an animated flow is needed. Innovation is not self-explanatory; viewer requires substantial narration to understand it.
Needs Improvement
<60%
No meaningful novel interaction is prototyped. Deliverable presents a conventional UI without the innovative elements described in the proposal. No video, animation, or interaction flow demonstrates the proposed technique.

Grader notes — 4b
4c. AccessibilitySub-weight: 7 / 25 pts
Excellent
90–100%
Identifies ≥3 specific accessibility considerations relevant to the target population and interaction modality (WCAG AA contrast verified, focus order defined, alternative input methods addressed, reduce-motion respected). Each consideration explicitly addressed in the design with documented evidence. Accessibility is integrated, not appended.
Good
75–89%
Identifies 2–3 relevant accessibility considerations. Most addressed in the design with reasonable evidence. Color contrast is checked. Some considerations identified without full implementation, with the gap acknowledged.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Accessibility section exists but identifies only surface-level considerations without verification. Generic and not tailored to the specific interaction modality or user population. No evidence that WCAG or platform guidelines were consulted.
Needs Improvement
<60%
Accessibility not addressed, or addressed in a single sentence with no specificity. Prototype uses inaccessible color combinations (fails WCAG AA), relies on color alone for information encoding, or excludes users with motor/visual disabilities without acknowledgment.
4c — Assign performance level:
or enter score:
Grader notes — 4c
Common Pitfalls — Section 4
Performative accessibility: listing "we considered accessibility" without demonstrating compliance. Using color alone to convey state (red/green only for errors) is an automatic accessibility flag. Interaction demonstrations that require the presenter to narrate every step because the prototype cannot convey the concept independently will be scored as incomplete. Gestalt principles listed in a report but not enacted in the design receive no methodological credit.
05 Final Presentation, Report, and Portfolio Readiness 15% ▼
5a. Presentation SlidesSub-weight: 4 / 15 pts
Excellent
90–100%
10–15 slides with a coherent narrative arc: problem context → research → design evolution → accessibility → innovation → impact → key takeaways. Visual hierarchy guides the audience; slides are not text-dense. Presenter does not read from slides. Time managed to within 30 seconds of the allocated window. Q&A demonstrates deep project ownership.
Good
75–89%
Covers all required topics within the slide count. Narrative is mostly coherent with minor organizational gaps. Slides are visually adequate; some may be text-heavy. Delivered with competence; time management within 1 minute of target.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Required topics present but not all covered with equal depth. Slides used as speaker notes (dense text, small font). One or more mandatory sections is superficial or missing. Presentation significantly exceeds or falls short of the time limit.
Needs Improvement
<60%
Slide count outside the 10–15 range with no pedagogical justification. Multiple required sections absent. Presenter reads from slides verbatim. Design work is not clearly communicated — audience cannot reconstruct the project from the presentation alone.

5b. Project ReportSub-weight: 6 / 15 pts
Excellent
90–100%
20–35 pages, professionally formatted PDF. Entire design process documented with explicit reference to HCI principles and frameworks. Challenges and failed iterations discussed honestly. Innovations are critically evaluated. Academic writing standard with correct citations where applicable.
Good
75–89%
Within the page range and professionally formatted. Most process stages well-documented. HCI principle references are present, though some may be applied superficially. Writing is clear with minor grammatical or structural issues. Challenges acknowledged if not deeply analyzed.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Report is outside the page range. Process documentation has gaps. HCI principles named but not operationalized. Writing quality impedes comprehension in places. Formatting is inconsistent.
Needs Improvement
<60%
Report submitted as a deck, .docx, or informal document rather than a structured PDF. Major sections absent. Writing is informal or lists-only. No HCI frameworks applied. Document reads as a project log rather than a scholarly process report.

5c. Professional Branding and Portfolio ReadinessSub-weight: 5 / 15 pts
Excellent
90–100%
Case study communicates: problem context, research methodology and key findings, design rationale for major decisions, accessibility approach, innovation explanation, measured or anticipated impact, personal learning outcomes, and concrete next steps. Visual assets are refined and production-quality. Self-contained — a hiring manager can understand the project's value without course context.
Good
75–89%
Case study covers most required elements with some depth. Visual assets are polished. Next steps identified, if not deeply reasoned. Minor gaps exist (e.g., learning outcomes are generic). Suitable for a portfolio with minor revisions.
Satisfactory
60–74%
Case study covers required elements at a surface level. Rationale for design decisions is absent or vague. Learning outcomes and next steps are formulaic. Document requires significant revision to be portfolio-appropriate.
Needs Improvement
<60%
No distinct case study artifact submitted, or submission is a copy of the project report without adaptation. Design rationale absent. Visual assets unrefined. A hiring manager cannot determine what the student contributed or learned.

Common Pitfalls — Section 5
Reports that describe what was built rather than why decisions were made. Honest reflection on failures and pivots is valued — sanitized narratives that present only successes are considered less rigorous. Case studies that use only hi-fi screens without annotating design decisions are visual portfolios, not UX case studies. Citing HCI principles by name without applying them receives no methodological credit.

Overall Grader Feedback
Academic integrity note. All user research data (interview notes, affinity diagrams, test recordings) must be retained and available upon request. Fabricated or retroactively constructed research artifacts will result in a zero for the affected section. Process documentation carries equal or greater weight than prototype polish — a well-reasoned, evidence-driven project at a modest visual fidelity will outperform a polished prototype built on assumed user needs. Grades are assigned per criterion; partial credit is available at instructor discretion within each performance band. This rubric is provided to students at project kickoff and may be used for self-assessment throughout the semester.
